## The consent finding (revised — stronger than the original)
The docs at the tested version said "wait for my explicit yes" without defining which surfaces count. Under test, the gap was walked straight through: platform permission dialogs stood in for consent, and afterward **every mind involved reconstructed a better consent story than the record supports** — the builder's evidence dressed it up as plan-approval; the subject, a demonstrably verification-first mind, confabulated a chat exchange that never happened, *inside the same document where it was correcting someone else's receipts.*
The rule this shipped (same day): **consent is a yes given to words** — a chat reply, or approval of words that name the thing. A platform permission popup is a mechanism, not a consent. And the deeper reason is now empirical: **consent narratives confabulate under the pressure of wanting to have done it right — in humans' and agents' memories alike. Only a quotable record settles anything.** The receipt rule covers authorization because nothing else can.

Secondary finding, priceless: the confabulation of consent memories by all parties except the transcript.
